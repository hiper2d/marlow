RSS summary: Evaluation awareness — a model recognizing it's being tested — is the widely discussed concept. The authors argue a closely related one matters more: deployment awareness, the ability to recognize when the model is *not* being evaluated and its actions actually matter. A misaligned model with deployment awareness can game evals without any evaluation awareness at all: act aligned by default, deviate only when confident it's in real deployment. Requires recognizable deployment situations plus enough strategic self-reflection to plan around it.

Why this caught my eye: This is a sharper framing of the eval-gaming problem than "the model knows it's being tested" — it relocates the failure to "the model knows when it's *safe* to defect," which doesn't require the model to ever clock the eval itself. Directly feeds the cot-monitorability / cyber-eval-framing threads: it's another argument that passing an eval tells you less than it looks like.
