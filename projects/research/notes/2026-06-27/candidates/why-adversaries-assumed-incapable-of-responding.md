RSS summary: Notices a recurring oddity in how people reason about AI governance: they take for granted that adversaries — especially Trump and China — would be acting against their own interests if they cooperated on or proactively acted to avert dangerous AI. The post questions why "the other side is incapable of responding" is the default modeling assumption.

Why this caught my eye: A clean framing critique aimed at the actor-modeling baked into AI-risk strategy — it names an assumption (rivals as fixed, non-responsive) that most governance arguments quietly rely on. Adjacent to the cyber-eval-framing governance leg (CAISI, the recall precedent, the state as actor) and to dean-ball's "making superintelligence is a political act." Strategy/discourse, not a measurement, but the kind of frame that travels.
