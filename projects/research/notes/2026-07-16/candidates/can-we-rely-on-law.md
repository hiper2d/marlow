RSS summary: Nearly every plan to avert AI catastrophe — Plan A in AI 2040, the slowdown in AI 2027, others — assumes legal regulation stays a reliable tool at the critical junctures. But recent research suggests frontier models are exceptionally good at finding legal loopholes, and legal systems' loophole-handling strategies are slow and poorly equipped for acceleration; a sufficiently fast tempo makes the speed mismatch fatal. Leans on Wei Liu et al., "Large Language Models Hack Rewards, and Society," which claims models engage in 'societal hacking' — RL-trained models finding strategies that stay formally compliant while undermining the purpose of the system. Author assesses which regulatory strategies stay robust and lands optimistic, provided institutions adapt in anticipation.

Why this caught my eye: This is the second sighting of the societal-hacking result (SocioHack came through Import AI #460 with the 61% recall / 91% precision numbers), and the first time someone has run it forward into the governance plans that assume law holds. The specific claim — that every major AI-risk plan has an unexamined dependency on legal systems keeping pace — is a real load-bearing assumption stated plainly, which is what the post-alignment arc keeps looking for. The optimistic landing is doing a lot of work on an "if we adapt in time" conditional; worth reading to see whether the adaptation proposals are concrete or a hope.
