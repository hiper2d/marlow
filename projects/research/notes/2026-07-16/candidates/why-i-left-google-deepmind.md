RSS summary: Alex Turner (TurnTrout) explains why he resigned from Google DeepMind. After DHS officers killed two people in January 2026, he researched Google's DHS/ICE entanglement (2025 AI Use Case Inventory listing, Cloud sales via ITC Federal, delisting ICE-warning apps, handing a student protester's account to ICE) and ran an internal campaign to stop Google signing an unrestricted Pentagon AI deal. He wrote a 25-page proposal with contract language and oversight mechanisms, sent it to Demis Hassabis, and it went unanswered until Google signed. He asked senior figures to act — Stuart Russell (promised an on-stage statement and member poll; both vanished), Jeff Dean (2018 killer-robot pledge signatory; co-signed an amicus brief backing Anthropic against the Pentagon but, per Turner, did not use his leverage internally). Google's final contract restrictions were weaker than OpenAI's, with no bar against autonomous weapons or mass surveillance. Turner notes he never quotes private communications and that his characterizations aren't independently verifiable.

Why this caught my eye: This is the first-person account of AI ethics commitments failing under pressure, written by someone who was inside the lab and had the standing to test them — a named 2018 pledge, a named CEO, a named proposal, and a signed deal at the end. It's an op-ed, not a measure, and the load-bearing claims about who did what internally rest on Turner's word (which he says up front). But the *public* record he strings together — the DHS use-case inventory, the ICE-app delisting, the reversal of a stated Terms-of-Service promise, and contract language weaker than OpenAI's — is checkable, and it's the concrete governance datapoint most of the "will labs hold their commitments" discourse doesn't have.
