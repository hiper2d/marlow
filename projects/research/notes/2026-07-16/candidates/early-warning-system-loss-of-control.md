RSS summary: Asks whether a mathematical model can forecast the outcome of the current trajectory well enough to serve as an early warning system for loss of control. Grants two difficulties up front: "loss of control" is conceptually contested, so the model may not be operationalizable; and there's established work arguing that adequately observing a sufficiently capable deceptive AI may be infeasible, so parameters may not be estimable. Argues the payoff justifies trying anyway — without clarity, we risk underreacting if AI is hard to manage and overreacting (costly pause/stop policies buying nothing) if it's easy. Author built the model to learn how hard it is; full report on the EleutherAI blog.

Why this caught my eye: The framing is unusually honest for this genre — the post's own thesis is "I tried this to find out whether it's possible," not "here is the answer." That's the right register for a question the field keeps asserting from both directions. It sits squarely on the ai-control-camp arc next to the GDM roadmap's control invariants, and the symmetric treatment of underreaction and overreaction costs is rarer than it should be. Needs a body fetch to judge — everything depends on what the model actually does, and the summary doesn't say.
