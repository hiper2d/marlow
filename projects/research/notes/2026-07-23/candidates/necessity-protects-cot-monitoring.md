RSS summary: A BlueDot Impact 5-week project. The case for reading CoT is that it's cheap, scalable, and simple; the case against is no guarantee the text is the reason. The author looks for a boundary by splitting tasks into ones where reasoning is load-bearing enough to trust the trace and ones where it isn't — framing monitorability as a property of task *necessity* (prevention) rather than the model choosing to disclose.

Why this caught my eye: This is the same load-bearing claim as the -16 `cots-monitorable-when-unfaithful-requires-computation` pick, arrived at independently — when a task genuinely requires the computation to happen in the trace, the monitor works by *prevention*, not disclosure; where it doesn't, disclosure is optional and vanishable. Third sighting in a week of the field converging on "monitorability is per-task, not universal," which is exactly the wedge under the GDM Control Roadmap's D1 placement of CoT monitoring. Independent (student project, not a lab) → real source diversity on an arc going monocrop. Direct `cot-monitorability` anchor, also `ai-control-camp`. Body-fetch worth it — the interesting part is where they drew the boundary and whether it holds empirically.
