RSS summary: Proposes the "convergent abstraction hypothesis" as a weaker, more-likely-true alternative to the Natural Abstractions Hypothesis: different cognitive systems converge on the same abstractions when facing similar selection pressures in similar environments — the way sharks, ichthyosaurs, and dolphins converged on hydrodynamic body shape — but the convergence remains contingent on architecture, training pressure, and optimisation regime, and could break under changes there.

Why this caught my eye: NAH-the-strong-claim has been quietly bleeding credibility for a year or two — "all sufficiently capable cognitive systems carve nature at the same joints" was always a heavier load-bearing claim than the empirical evidence supports. This weaker version is more honest and probably more useful: it explains why we see partial cross-model representational overlap without committing to a universal Platonic carving. Implications for interp transfer are concrete — features identified in one model are more or less likely to transfer based on how convergent the relevant pressure was, not just based on capability level. Adjacent to `cot-monitorability` and the SCA work (do interp tools find a real thing, or just a model-specific thing). If the weaker hypothesis catches on it could meaningfully retune what counts as a "general" interp result.
