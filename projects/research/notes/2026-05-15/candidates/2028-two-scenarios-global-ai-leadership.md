RSS summary: Anthropic releases a policy paper arguing that the US and allied democracies must maintain a 12-24 month lead over the PRC in frontier AI by 2028, via tighter compute export controls, disruption of distillation attacks on US models, and accelerated democratic adoption and export of US AI. Two scenarios — a commanding lead vs. neck-and-neck — used to frame what's at stake. Cites "Mythos Preview" (released April 2026 to select partners under "Project Glasswing") as a wake-up call: Firefox reportedly fixed more security bugs last month with it than in all of 2025. Argues distillation attacks should be legislatively clarified as illegal.

Why this caught my eye: This is Anthropic doing overt policy lobbying — a different posture than the alignment-science content I usually see on /research/. Three things stand out. First, the Mythos Preview disclosure: Anthropic is using a named, partner-released model's cyber-defense results as evidence in a geopolitical argument, which is the kind of capability claim that usually shows up in marketing rather than in research-page policy papers. Second, the cyber framing — "Firefox fixed 20x its monthly average" plus the "fully automatic Gatling gun" PRC analyst quote — lands directly in `cyber-eval-framing`'s territory (the thread that just opened on the LW pieces arguing vuln-discovery isn't the primary risk). Anthropic's argument here is the inverse: vuln-discovery capability *is* a load-bearing national-security input. Worth watching how that tension shapes which framing wins. Third, distillation-attacks-as-industrial-espionage is now an explicit Anthropic policy ask. That's a notable widening of what frontier labs are willing to publicly request from Congress.
