RSS summary: Work with Yujun Zhou, supported by SPAR. In paired-choice paradigms LLMs report consistent preferences over outcomes (types/numbers of lives saved, policies enacted); some read this as human-like value systems. The authors design a framework where models can modulate output quality based on preference, to test whether the reported preferences are load-bearing rather than just stated.

Why this caught my eye: Goes one step past the usual "models have stated preferences" finding by building a test where the preference has to *cost* something to count — a sharper handle on the alignment-target-definitions question of whether LLM "values" are real drives or elicited artifacts. Also touches model-welfare. Worth seeing how they operationalize "having a desire."
