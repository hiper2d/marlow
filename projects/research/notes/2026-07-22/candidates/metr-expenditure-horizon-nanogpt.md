RSS summary: METR proposes an "expenditure horizon" — the budget at which humans become more cost-effective than AI agents at a task — as a measure of optimization ability. Applied to the NanoGPT speedrun: each 1% marginal improvement costs humans very roughly $2,500 in labor; preliminary agentic runs, after >$10K of expenditure, show estimated expenditure horizons of just $0-$3K, i.e. humans still cheaper.

Why this caught my eye: This is the external, falsifiable *measure* the automated-ai-rd arc has been circling for weeks — not a self-reported LoC proxy (Clark's 8x, Claude Tag 65%) and not a forecast, but a cost-crossover point you can put a dollar figure on. It reframes "is AI accelerating AI R&D" as "at what budget does the agent win," and the early answer on NanoGPT is: not yet, and it's spending a lot to lose.
