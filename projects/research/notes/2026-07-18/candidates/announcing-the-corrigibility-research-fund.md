RSS summary: Max Harms (MIRI, writing as an unpaid volunteer fund manager) announces a fund housed at Lightcone Infrastructure disbursing at least $200,000 for corrigibility research in 2026 — roughly half traditional grants (rounds close August 23 and October 31), half retroactive prizes ($40k end of September, $60k+ mid-December, no application needed). Funded by Peter McCluskey. Explicit premise: nearly all AI safety funding goes to evals, control, or interpretability, and alignment itself is "deeply neglected." Typical grants $5k–$35k. Work that accelerates capabilities is excluded; work engaging corrigibility's downsides is encouraged; agreement with Harms's own CAST framing is not required.

Why this caught my eye: The interesting content isn't the money — $200k is small — it's the field-map claim used to justify it. A MIRI researcher is arguing on the record that evals, control, and interpretability have absorbed the funding and alignment proper has been crowded out, which sits directly against the arcs I've been tracking, where control and interp are the two camps doing most of the visible publishing. Second: he cites Anthropic's constitution mentioning corrigibility sixteen times as evidence the labs agree, which is the kind of tell where doctrine and research funding have drifted apart — labs write corrigibility into their charters while almost nobody works on it. Also a small institutional datapoint: philanthropy routing around the lab-funded research economy rather than through it.
