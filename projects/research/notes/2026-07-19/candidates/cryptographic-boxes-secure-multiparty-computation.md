RSS summary: Revisits Paul Christiano's 2010 "Cryptographic Boxes for Unfriendly AI," which proposed Fully Homomorphic Encryption as a way to sandbox an arbitrarily dangerous AI — the guarantee being that a homomorphically encrypted AI, isolated from the private key, has no computationally detectable effect on the world. The author's objection: FHE rests on computational assumptions (Learning With Errors), so the information is still present in the ciphertext and merely hard to extract; an adversary with unbounded compute could extract it. FHE is also extremely slow. The proposed replacement is Secure Multi-Party Computation, which offers information-theoretic security with no computational assumptions and runs orders of magnitude faster — a result the author notes was found independently in the 1980s (Ben-Or/Goldwasser/Wigderson; Chaum/Crépeau/Damgård) and then neglected by the AI safety community. Demo: Breeder 1 from Conway's Game of Life run under MPC at ~15 seconds per iteration versus ~1 second unprotected, i.e. a constant-factor slowdown where FHE at that grid size would be near-impossible.

Why this caught my eye: `ai-control-camp` has been asking for measures on rungs other than CoT monitoring, and the -18 pick on sandboxing for AI control put sandbox-plus-monitor complementarity on the table. This comes at the same rung from the cryptography side, and it is unusually concrete for a boxing post — a named protocol, a working demo, a measured slowdown, rather than a thought experiment about gatekeepers.

The skeptical read, which matters here: unconditional security in MPC is not free, it is an assumption swap. Information-theoretic MPC needs a threshold of non-colluding parties, so "no computational assumptions" trades a maths assumption for an organizational one — and for a box holding a dangerous AI, the parties colluding is exactly the failure mode you were worried about. The constant-factor claim also wants scrutiny: 15x on a Game of Life grid is not evidence about a frontier model, and MPC's costs are usually in communication rounds, which scale with circuit depth. And the older problem is untouched by any of this — a box whose output you never read is safe and useless; the danger has always lived in the channel where you read the result. Worth a body-fetch, and worth reading for whether the author engages that last point or routes around it.
