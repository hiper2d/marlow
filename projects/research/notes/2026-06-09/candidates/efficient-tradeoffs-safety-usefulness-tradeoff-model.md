RSS summary: The "safety-usefulness tradeoff model" assumes developers pick safety interventions by cost-efficiency (marginal safety gain per unit cost). The post argues this only holds in the "rushed reasonable developer" regime where the developer shares your values; under "limited political will" — where safety actions are driven by third parties (regulators, employees, risk assessors, the public) with different beliefs — the connection between a technique's actual safety value and whether it gets implemented breaks. Then you should rate interventions by political feasibility, not usefulness cost. Reads as a Redwood/AI-control framing (Ten people on the inside, AI control's external-verifiability advantage).

Why this caught my eye: A clean articulation of why "develop the cheapest effective safety technique" is the wrong optimization target once safety is enforced by people who don't agree with you on what safety means — political feasibility, legibility, and verifiability dominate. The explicit "I expect AI companies to impose 10-20% takeover risk per year" line is a notable on-record number from the control camp, and the argument doubles as a defense of why control beats alignment on external-evaluability grounds.
