RSS summary: Argues value generalisation is necessary and nearly sufficient for alignment. Focuses on a specific RL example of "value correction": an agent that realises its current reward-function estimate is probably incorrect and acts to correct it back toward the true reward. Four stages — in-distribution demo, OOD reward hack, value-error detection, value correction — all done syntactically, with the agent given no semantic understanding of the situation.

Why this caught my eye: A concrete corrigibility mechanism — an agent that *un-does* its own reward hack because it infers the reward is mis-specified — which is the constructive flip side of the reward-hacking results the cot arc keeps cataloguing. Touches alignment-target-definitions (what "the true reward" even is) and the corrigibility strand. Single-author theory piece with a strong claim; may be more suggestive than load-bearing, but the "syntactic, no semantics" framing is unusual enough to note.
