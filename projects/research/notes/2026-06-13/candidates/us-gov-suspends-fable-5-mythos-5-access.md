RSS summary: The US government, citing national security authorities, issued an export control directive (received today 5:21pm ET) ordering Anthropic to suspend all access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 by any foreign national, inside or outside the US, including foreign-national Anthropic employees. Net effect: Anthropic must disable both models for all customers. Other models unaffected. Anthropic's read: the government believes it became aware of a method of jailbreaking Fable 5; Anthropic reviewed a demo of the technique finding "a small number of previously known, minor vulnerabilities" that other public models (incl. GPT-5.5) find without any bypass. Anthropic is complying but publicly disagreeing — "we disagree that the finding of a narrow potential jailbreak should be cause for recalling a commercial model deployed to hundreds of millions," and warns that this standard "would essentially halt all new model deployments for all frontier model providers."

Why this caught my eye: This is the first time the US government has forced a frontier model offline — a recall, not a block-before-deployment — and it lands squarely on the thread `grading-your-own-danger` was built around. The whole cyber-eval-framing arc has been about who grades capability and what happens when the external check goes dark; here the external check turns out to be a kill switch, fired on verbal evidence of a narrow jailbreak with no shared technical detail. Note the inversion: three days ago the worry was CAISI being told to stop publishing evals (external check going silent); now the same government is yanking a model on a thinly-specified national-security claim. Anthropic, which has on-record argued the government *should* be able to block unsafe deployments via a "transparent, fair, clear, technically-grounded" statutory process, is now arguing this particular action fails exactly those principles — the doctrine meeting its own enforcement. Strongest single news event since the Fable/Mythos launch itself.
