RSS summary: Steven Byrnes' idiosyncratic take on the egregious-misalignment debate. One side (Yudkowsky & Soares): if AI progress continues we're on a direct path to scheming, out-of-control, not-even-slightly-nice ASI absent breakthrough alignment ideas. Other side (≈everyone who works on LLMs): variously concerned, but if worried about scheming they'd route it through race dynamics / careless programmers / bad actors rather than the simpler "nobody has any idea how to avoid it." Byrnes argues BOTH are true: (1) reasoning carefully about ASI properties gives good reason to expect egregious misalignment absent new ideas; (2) reasoning carefully about *current LLMs* gives good reason to think otherwise — the two camps are reasoning about different objects.

Why this caught my eye: Byrnes is already a tracked voice in alignment-target-definitions (empowerment-corrigibility), and this is the clearest attempt I've seen to dissolve the doomer/empiricist split by pointing at *which object each side reasons about* — ASI-in-principle vs LLMs-as-they-are. That's a sharper frame than the usual talking-past-each-other, and it's the kind of camp-mapping a blog piece could hang a through-line on if a second anchor shows up.
