RSS summary: Asterisk magazine interview asking when AI becomes "self-sustaining" — Cotra's definition: AI integrated with physical infrastructure (factories, mines, fabs, robots) such that it needs no cognitive or physical human inputs to keep growing its own population. Ajeya Cotra (METR): plausible within 10 years (by 2036). Timothy B. Lee (journalist): <10% within 20 years, median ~50 years, 10-20% never. Crux is humanoid robots — hands, manufacturing rate, cost, repairability. Bottleneck debate: Lee's tacit-knowledge argument (the fabs would take decades to restart without the people) vs Cotra's two counters (RL trained on that tacit knowledge; or generally-intelligent systems that learn new things fast). Clark's frame: self-sustaining AI is a measure of "the declining leverage that humans have in negotiating with synthetic intelligences."

Why this caught my eye: This is the "what comes *after* RSI" question, and it reframes takeover risk as an embodiment/logistics problem rather than a cognition one — human redundancy requires hands and supply chains, not just smarter models. The tacit-knowledge crux is the genuinely contestable bit and a good hook: it's the same argument that shows up in deindustrialization and offshoring debates, transplanted onto AI. Feeds automated-ai-rd as the upper bound of the RSI arc, and the "declining human leverage" framing is a sharper, more falsifiable handle on disempowerment than the usual doom register. Pairs with the Recursive and DeepMind ASI candidates from this issue.
