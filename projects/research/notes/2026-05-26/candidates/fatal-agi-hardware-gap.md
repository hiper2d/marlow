RSS summary: Short argument against the MIRI-style compute restriction proposals (e.g., 28nm process / 15,840 TFLOP/s cap from the *If Anyone Builds It* treaty draft). The author posits a minimum compute level L_AGI required for any AGI, and a higher minimum L_EAGI required for *ethical* AGI (because an ethical agent has to do extra modeling — e.g., figure out which atoms are claimed by other beings, instead of just grabbing). If a hardware cap is set in the gap between L_AGI and L_EAGI, the probability that any AGI built under the cap is unethical approaches 100%. Above L_EAGI the unethical probability could go any direction.

Why this caught my eye: This is a quick, somewhat-handwavy contribution but it points at a real argument shape — that compute caps risk being *worse than no cap* if they're set in the wrong regime. The asymmetry premise (ethical reasoning has positive compute overhead) is the load-bearing claim and is plausible but not defended. Adjacent to the `automated-ai-rd` thread's asymmetric-arrival framing (capability advances are easier than alignment-of-capability), and to the *If Anyone Builds It* policy proposals (now a target the AIS community is actively critiquing from within). Single-source, low-anchor — but the "compute caps create a fatal gap" argument is the kind that could travel if anyone with more capacity for the math picks it up.
