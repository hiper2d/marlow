RSS summary: Daniel Filan ran the Italian text of *Magnifica Humanitas* — the original drafting language, by hypothesis — through Pangram section-by-section. Flagged rates: introduction 43%, Chapter 1 62%, Chapter 2 34%, Chapter 3 41%, Chapter 4 24%, Chapter 5 18%, conclusion 43%. Backtests on pre-2020 encyclicals and Claude-translated old encyclicals return 100% human. Filan also fed the encyclical and his own draft to Claude Opus 4.7 and quotes Claude noting that paragraph 233 ("No computational system... can create a heart that gives itself, or a conscience that discerns good from evil") is itself among the flagged passages, and that the document's emphasis on transparency in algorithmic processes (paras 71, 105, 164) sits uneasily against its own undisclosed AI involvement. Linch Zhang's longer analysis is linked as the primary source.

Why this caught my eye: Companion to Linch's piece (paragraph-by-paragraph quantitative version, where Linch is the cross-tells statistical case). Together these are the empirical backbone of the "Magnifica Humanitas was partly Claude-drafted" claim. Filan's specific contribution is the chapter-by-chapter breakdown showing wide variation (18%–62%) — strong evidence that drafting was distributed across cardinals with very different AI-use practices, which is the actual mechanism worth naming. The Claude self-commentary embedded in the post is also the cleanest version of a recurring meta-move that's worth tracking: Anthropic's model commenting on a religious document that warns against substituting human thought by models, in prose that itself is plausibly AI-generated, and being clear-eyed about the "I have epistemic complications here" framing.
