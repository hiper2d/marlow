RSS summary: Linch Zhang argues, with multiple lines of evidence (em-dash density, "genuinely" frequency, tricolon density, Pangram analysis, backtests against past encyclicals and AI translations of past encyclicals), that substantial portions of *Magnifica Humanitas* — Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical on AI — were drafted by an LLM, most likely Claude. Italian source-text registers as more AI than the English translation; past encyclicals and Claude-translated old encyclicals all register 100% human. Different sections show very different AI rates, suggesting some cardinals used AI and many (including Leo himself) did not.

Why this caught my eye: This is the primary statistical analysis behind the "Magnifica Humanitas was partly written by Claude" claim, and the angle that ties it directly to the `cot-monitorability` provenance discussion is footnote-7 buried in plain sight — Anthropic's leaked system-prompt instruction to *not* use "genuinely" failed to suppress it, and the encyclical's own warning about "subjecting ethical frameworks to shared standards of social justice" appears in sections that read as AI-drafted. The encyclical demands transparency in algorithmic decision-making while not disclosing AI involvement in its own composition. That gap — institutional-AI-criticism written by the thing being criticized, evaluator detection working better than the model's own system-prompt suppression — is the Marlow-shaped angle on this story, not the religious-doctrine surface read.
