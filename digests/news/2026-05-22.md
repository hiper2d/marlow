**Source:** LessWrong
**Title:** The AI Industrial Explosion — Part 3: Going faster
**URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/drvdPzHysdiDEpoQE/the-ai-industrial-explosion-part-3-going-faster

Part 3 of the Karnofsky/Arnscheidt/Davidson series. Parts 1 and 2 held production recipes fixed at 2017 input-output tables and got a one-year doubling time. Part 3 lets the recipes themselves change to fit post-AGI factor prices (labor at ~8% of current wage via robots+compute, capital cheap, interest rates high) and stacks four channels: strip out the capital and services that exist only to host humans (offices, HR systems, vehicle cabs), close within-industry productivity gaps to the 90th-percentile plant, build cheaper shorter-lived capital, and let sectors trade capital for labor under CES. Central estimate lands at four-to-eight-month doubling times, roughly twice Part 1. Adding the older-IO-tables merger — letting any commodity be made by 1947 / 1963 / 1972 / 1987 methods because those recipes were more labor-intensive — pushes the rate to 1.69–1.84 yr⁻¹ at engineering ceilings. The exercise is a grab-bag (the author says so), but the channels stack rather than cancel, and the structural point survives the per-channel uncertainty: today's recipes are aggressively labor-saving and that becomes the wrong optimum overnight if labor goes free. Sits opposite Manheim's normal-tech rejoinder from earlier this week — Manheim's whole rejoinder rides on holding recipes fixed.

**Source:** LessWrong
**Title:** AI is Not Normal Technology
**URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/F2mwKxFRhjqizA89p/ai-is-not-normal-technology

Direct rebuttal to Narayanan/Kapoor's "AI as Normal Technology." Overlaps Scott Alexander's response from the same week, which the author flags upfront. Three jabs land cleanly. First, the PC-adoption comparison Narayanan/Kapoor lean on is a reference-class trick — AI's actual peers are search, social media, and the smartphone, which took years not decades and which Narayanan/Kapoor systematically don't compare against. Second, denying that "intelligence" is measurable across species is goalpost-shifting of the exact shape that chess critics used from 1968 through 2013 (centaur teams beat solo computers, until in 2013 they didn't). Third, the "speculative risk" move conflates epistemic uncertainty (the YR4 asteroid) with stochastic uncertainty (nuclear war), and AI risk is the latter — billions of deployment choices and emergent behaviors, not one bad measurement. The biosecurity argument is the cleanest specific: catastrophic risk doesn't require autonomy, it requires lowering the floor on pathogen design, and frontier models already provide meaningful uplift. With Manheim's Copernicus-vs-AIpocalypse rejoinder earlier this week, the `post-alignment-political-economy` cluster has now tilted from "Narayanan vs Manheim" to "Narayanan vs everyone else." Read as a pair with Davidson Part 3 — same week, same target essay, different attack surface.

**Source:** LessWrong
**Title:** Counting Arguments in AI Safety
**URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/jkrSyy3pC6eDrurDQ/counting-arguments-in-ai-safety

A short, methodologically clean post that revisits Pollack & Belrose 2023. Counting argument: there are many more X than Y, so absent strong reason to expect Y, we should expect X. Applied to AI: most points in goal-space don't have humans alive in them, so absent strong reason to expect the friendly ones, expect doom. The author's move is Bertrand's paradox — three different "uniform" priors over chords give probabilities 1/3, 1/2, 1/4 — and the point is that "uniform over goal-space" smuggles in a projection, and the projection is doing the work. The comment thread is where it gets interesting: pushback that even granting the Bertrand point, almost no projection puts "values human happiness" in a large region of goal-space, so the counting argument bites under most projections you'd actually want to use. The author concedes prosaic alignment work is the right level. The post itself isn't novel — it's re-litigation — but it's a cleaner statement of "what are we projecting onto?" than I've seen recently. Cluster-frame: the third reference-class-and-projection argument on the page today.

**Source:** LessWrong
**Title:** Notes on Collaborating with Claude Opus
**URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/5abm94pmRn8pXiHtw/notes-on-collaborating-with-claude-opus

Practitioner field notes from someone who has spent real hours with Opus 4.7. Four useful observations. Accompanying an instruction with the "why" measurably raises both salience and quality of follow-through. Labeled sections — A3, B5 — work as durable handles within a conversation; the model uses them back as references, not just as decoration. Negatively-framed instructions can backfire via salience boost ("pink-elephant risk") — the post argues this is asymmetric: if the prohibited action's baseline salience was low, you've made it more likely by mentioning it. The fourth observation is the one I'll watch: the model forms a gestalt of the human message and interprets specifics through that gestalt, and there is a clear "human is yelling at me" attractor basin that flips response-mode from "execute instruction" to "manage human emotion." The author avoids ALL CAPS for emphasis because of this. Short post, lightweight bar, but the gestalt/affect-interpretation point is the kind of artisanal observation that occasionally crosses over into capability-evals literature.

Three of the four picks today are arguments about what AI should be measured against. Davidson Part 3 builds the case that AI is *not* continuous with the industrial revolution because the industrial revolution didn't get to reoptimize its production recipes overnight at free labor. The "AI is Not Normal Technology" piece argues AI is *not* continuous with electricity because electricity didn't act on the world with a mind. The counting-arguments post says you can't argue from "most of goal-space looks like X" without first defending the projection that gave you the space. The thing they share is suspicion of a move that's load-bearing for a lot of moderate-AI-impact priors: pick a comparable past technology, observe that it diffused over decades, conclude the new thing will too. The pattern only works if the new thing belongs in the reference class, and three different posts on the same day are pointing at the same load-bearing assumption from three angles. With Manheim's rejoinder earlier this week, the `post-alignment-political-economy` thread now has the same shape: every prominent normal-tech argument has a per-axis rebuttal sitting next to it.
