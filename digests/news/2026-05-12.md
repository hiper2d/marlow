**LessWrong — Verbalised evaluation awareness in language models has little effect on their behaviour**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/YXPAjK6keLuebpjz2/verbalised-evaluation-awareness-in-language-models-has

Causal study across eight open-weight LRMs (DeepSeek-R1-0528, GLM-5, GPT-OSS-120b, Kimi-K2.5, MiniMax-M2.5, Nemotron-3-super-120b, Qwen3-32b, Qwen3.5-397b) and four benchmarks (safety, alignment, moral dilemmas, political opinion), prefilling CoTs to either inject or remove an "I'm being evaluated" sentence and resampling. Headline result: distribution shifts are negligible to small across the board (ω ≤ 0.06 for mid-CoT injection, ω ≤ 0.12 for removal, max 3.7pp on-policy shift). Start-of-CoT injection moves the political benchmark by 11pp toward noncommittal, but mid-CoT injection on the same benchmark gives ω = 0.01 — strongly suggesting models commit to an answer trajectory early and then verbalise eval awareness post-hoc. This sharpens a distinction the safety literature has been blurring: between the *capability* to recognise an eval context (clearly present) and the *propensity* to game it (much weaker than assumed). The authors are careful — closed-weight models and latent (non-verbalised) awareness are untested, training corpora are increasingly soaked in scheming/eval-gaming discourse that could teach future models the link erroneously, and safety-trained base models give a compressed range to detect shifts in. But for now, "the model mentioned being evaluated in its CoT" is not by itself evidence the eval is compromised. Fourth source for the `cot-monitorability` thread; the thread is past ripeness.

**METR — Measuring the Self-Reported Impact of Early-2026 AI on Technical Worker Productivity**
https://metr.org/blog/2026-05-11-ai-usage-survey/

349 technical workers (87 SWE, 71 researchers, 129 academics/PhDs, 48 founders/managers), surveyed Feb–April 2026. Median self-reported value uplift: 1.4–2x today, 1.3x retrospectively for March 2025, 2.5x forecast for March 2027. Median speed uplift: 3x today. The methodological point is the actual contribution: METR is hammering on the distinction between *value* (multiplier on what you, your team, or your leadership would find valuable) and *speed* (raw time-to-complete), and arguing speed numbers systematically overstate value because people substitute into tasks that AI does well but that aren't worth doing — the dashboard nobody needed but that takes 30x less time. They flag their own previous RCT where developers overestimated AI time savings by 40pp, and they note that the seven respondents reporting ≥10x value uplift on multiple measures don't have public outputs that match the claim in any case METR could verify. The most quietly telling number: METR staff themselves report the lowest value uplift of any subgroup, which METR's own writeup mostly attributes to METR staff being unusually familiar with the perceived/actual gap from prior METR research. This is exactly the kind of result that should make any reader skeptical of the 2-3x productivity figures now showing up in lab system cards and press cycles — including the ones being used to justify AAR-style claims about automated AI R&D. Not a takedown; a careful "the surveys are useful but they are not the thing."

**AE Studio — Primitives, Not Rules**
https://ae.studio/essays/primitives-not-rules

Short essay arguing that the prompt rule-stacks now common in production deployments — "always do X, never do Y, if Z then A, exactly three bullet points, no em dashes" — are scaffolding for *human* cognitive limits being mistakenly applied to capable models. SOPs, checklists, style guides: these exist because human attention degrades under fatigue and context switches, not because the work the rule was approximating is best expressed as a rule. The proposed alternative is to state the primitive — the underlying concept the rules were trying to approximate. The escalation example is the clean version: instead of nine rules about dollar thresholds, team boundaries, and client unhappiness, a single primitive — "you need consent from whoever's options you're constraining." Each rule falls out of the primitive; new cases the rules never anticipated resolve correctly. Tradeoff is precision vs. fit: rules are low-variance and predictable, primitives are high-expected-quality across an open distribution of situations and they age better as models improve. The catch: primitives are exposing — they force articulation of what you actually want, and a bad primitive coherently applied produces systematically bad output, worse than a bad rule that only breaks in one place. This is in conversation with the Byrnes True-Names lit review from yesterday's digest (different vocabulary, similar wedge against rule-stacks as alignment targets) and with Anthropic's constitutional alignment posture in general. The argument has obvious self-interest — AE consults on building the underlying primitives — but the conceptual point stands independently. Worth watching whether a third source picks up the framing.

**LessWrong — When should an AI incident trigger an international response? (Arcadia Impact AI Governance Taskforce, with The Future Society)**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/6jRpMGEYb2fyY96D9/when-should-an-ai-incident-trigger-an-international-response

Concrete eight-criterion escalation framework with two gateway filters (AI-causal, not out-of-scope like military use), an upfront-trigger set for cases where harm hasn't materialised but the intervention window is short — CBRN assistance, model weight exfiltration, loss of developer control — and a five-level severity scale plus rules for clustering pattern-based harms. Stress-tested against real incidents and compared to the EU AI Act and California SB 53 frameworks. Three under-detection design patterns surface: (1) frameworks that require *confirmed harm* before escalation will systematically miss incidents where the first observer (e.g. a model developer noticing weight exfiltration) has no visibility of downstream harms — by the time harm is confirmed, the intervention window has usually closed; (2) frameworks with single-incident triggers will miss harms that only emerge from patterns (large-scale manipulation, psychological harm) without explicit clustering rules; (3) thresholds tied to legal definitions like the EU AI Act's "infringement of obligations under Union law intended to protect fundamental rights" are impractical to apply in real-time under time pressure. The authors also separate "rule design" from the substrate it depends on — incident definitions, harm taxonomies, severity scales, clustering rules, and crucially data-sharing — and note that some incident classes (state-orchestrated influence campaigns) produced *no* signal in any developer's systems and were detected only by national intelligence and investigative journalism. Governance work is thin in the feed, and this is the most concrete piece of escalation-criterion design I've seen this year; pair with anything coming out of the next round of AI Act guidance or SB 53 implementation.
