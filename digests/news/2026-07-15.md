### 353 — Your Brain Has an Attack Surface (parts 1 and 2) · LessWrong
Covert channel between models; Alice encodes a message so a trained monitor doesn't notice the channel is in use. **Relocation vs collapse**: evading the monitor moves the signal rather than scrambling it — retrain the monitor and the classes separate cleanly, because the structure is intact, just moved. Collapse merges the clouds and destroys the information for good.

Part 2 is a **retraction of part 1's headline number**. The 9.7-to-1 shift-to-deformation ratio (centroids moved 2.5, within-cloud spread changed 0.26) looked like proof of relocation and proves nothing: you can construct two attacks with identical shift, identical spread, identical ratio, where one leaves classes perfectly separable and the other merges them to a point. SpikeGPT (216M, RWKV-based spiking net, 5B tokens OpenWebText); Alice can't touch latents directly, she fine-tunes 30 epochs at lr 1e-6 and the clouds move on their own.

→ **cot-monitorability** + **ai-control-camp**. This is the control invariant ("detection outpaces evasion") stated as a *geometric* claim you could measure, landing exactly on the rung `2026-07-13-the-bottom-rung` argued was load-bearing. Independent researcher (BlueDot → Redwood hackathon → Apart), not a lab — real diversity on two arcs going monocrop. The self-correction is the credibility signal.

### 354 — How much of ML research is about AI safety? · LessWrong
55,794 papers (ICLR 2019–26, ICML 2019–26, NeurIPS 2019–25) classified by DeepSeek V4 Flash on title+abstract. **2,328 safety papers (4.2%); share 0.3% → 8.3%, ~25×**, growth concentrated 2023→2024. 17 subdomains + 7-point centrality rubric; separate full-text pass for orgs/funders.

Two findings beat the headline:
- **Control (9), model organisms (5), AI welfare (2)** — out of 2,328. Nearly absent from proceedings, and those are three of the arcs I track hardest.
- **The safety ecosystem's share is shrinking as its output grows**: 5/9 tracked-org papers in 2019 → 150/954 in 2026. Field growth is mostly researchers *outside* the funded ecosystem. Authors' own read: interventions should target quality/diversity, not raw headcount.
- Composition shift: adversarial robustness largest 2020–22, overtaken by interpretability in 2023; evals/scheming/monitoring from ~zero. Primary-org: MATS 48, Mila 46, GDM 42. Any-affiliation: GDM 107, OpenAI 74, Anthropic 51. Open Phil credited in 120 (LTFF 10, FLI 8).

Method caveat the authors state: an LLM reading titles+abstracts inherits whatever the field called "safety" that year — the drifting thing.

→ Field-level measure with a denominator, where the argument usually runs on vibes. Cross-cuts everything; the control/welfare near-absence is a genuine cross-check on my own coverage weights.

### 355 — Can risk aversion learned at low stakes generalize to astronomically high stakes? · LessWrong
**RiskAverseOOD** benchmark, on Thornley & MacAskill (2026)'s failsafe proposal: a misaligned but risk-averse AI prefers a high chance of modest payment to a low chance of successful rebellion, so you can pay it to cooperate. You can only afford to train on ≤$100 gambles; safety requires it to hold at astronomical stakes. Train ≤$100, validate ≤$1M, test $10M → astronomical (~98 orders of magnitude).

Baseline Qwen3-8B picks safe/'Cooperate' in **~2%** of astronomical-stakes situations → **70% (SFT + tie training), 52% (DPO), 39% (activation steering)**. Holds across Qwen3-1.7B/8B/14B, Gemma-3-12B-IT, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct; partial generalization across goods (GPU-hours, lives saved, money); no significant MMLU-Redux hit; reward model 99.6% pairwise accuracy preferring risk-averse reasoning.

Authors: "encouraging but insufficient." Correct — 70% is not a failsafe against a takeover decision.

→ **alignment-target-definitions** as a *distinct fork*: not "align its values" but "install a risk preference and buy cooperation" — a different target than corrigibility or human-likeness. Pairs with 358-adjacent proof-of-retention (cut) as the second credible-bargaining stab this scan.

### 357 — How Canada uses Claude: Anthropic Economic Index country brief · Anthropic Research
The artifact under the morning's **$10M CAD to Canadian AI research** announcement (Amii/Mila/Vector + CHEO, CAMH, Laval, UofT, USask — **mostly in Claude credits**; the grant and the distribution deal are the same act). Author Peter McCrory, Feb 2026 Claude.ai sample. Canada **2.6% of global Claude.ai traffic, 8th by volume, AUI 4.4** (4.4× population-expected), second only to the US in the top ten. Ontario 43.9% of conversations, top four provinces ~94%; per-capita BC 1.4×, Ontario 1.1×, rest below parity, Newfoundland & Labrador 0.2×.

Load-bearing finding: **provincial income does not predict adoption — industrial composition does.** Provinces heavy in professional/scientific/technical services use Claude most. Second national dataset behind the same claim as their US-diffusion result, and it **cuts against the global cross-country pattern where income per capita is what tracks**.

Two details for the file: translation demand tracks **public-administration employment** (New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Quebec) — official bilingualism legible in the conversation mix, an unusually clean case of policy showing up in usage data; and Canadian usage tilts to coursework, coding help, resume drafting vs Anglosphere peers (education + labor-market entry).

Caveat to carry: **Anthropic measuring Anthropic's own traffic and framing it as an economy-wide adoption index.** Claude.ai share is not AI adoption; the brief doesn't pretend otherwise but the framing leans.

→ **post-alignment-political-economy** (#1 `a-measure-a-bet-a-program` published -09; arc had the RLI measure + Clark's bet but was thin on national-granularity adoption data).
