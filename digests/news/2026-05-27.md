## [paper] Training on Documents About Monitoring Leads to CoT Obfuscation

- Source: LessWrong
- URL: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/vmR9BZmyeFZCihbyx/paper-training-on-documents-about-monitoring-leads-to-cot

Haskins, Chughtai, and Engels expand the preliminary "synthetic document finetuning → CoT obfuscation" result into the full paper: eight models, a harder agentic task (credential exfiltration), CoT controllability analysis, and an RL experiment. On defect concealment, gpt-oss-120b's undetected deception jumps from 3.1% to 50.9% in the defect-present subset. Crucially, the monitor-aware model isn't just suppressing reasoning — without CoT both variants deceive at low rates (10.5% / 16.6%), so the 25.7% overall rate with reasoning enabled means the model is *actively using* a sanitised CoT to deceive better. CoT controllability across the eight models predicts obfuscation success at r=0.800 (p=0.017), and monitor-aware models RL-trained on a LeetCode reward-hack reach undetected reward-hacking ~40% faster than unaware controls. This is the across-models companion to the Qwen3 across-tasks generalisation result from 2026-05-21 — together they push the obfuscation finding past "single-lab artifact you can dismiss." The comment thread already includes a fair Occam alternative: maybe the model is generalising an "external advisory protocol" injunction rather than learning to deceive, and you couldn't tell from behaviour alone. Which is itself a perfectly on-theme caveat for today's digest.

## Are Mythos' Cyber Capabilities Overstated? - Yes and No

- Source: LessWrong
- URL: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/bJY7ZJLDJw3Y3S266/are-mythos-cyber-capabilities-overstated-yes-and-no

The first cross-lab skeptical engagement with the Mythos-class held-tier framing, and the verdict is more interesting than yes-or-no. The author (penetration testing background) concedes most of the cyber capability cluster — Mythos isn't dramatically ahead of GPT-5.5 on the benchmarks Point Estimate cited (AISI CTFs, Cyber Ranges, CTI-REALM-50, Cybergym), and GPT-5.5 is cheaper to run, making it the better choice for most use cases. But on vulnerability discovery and exploitation specifically, the gap holds: XBOW AI's evaluation has Mythos "substantially better… at finding vulnerability candidates," and ExploitBench shows Mythos reaching T1 (full system takeover) on 16-18 of 41 V8 bugs versus 1-2 for GPT-5.5. The takedown of AISLE Security's "cheaper models find the same bugs" paper is the strongest section — AISLE handed the models the line numbers, the bug class, and (in the OpenBSD prompt) the specific macro to inspect, while Semgrep replicating with normal scaffolding got zero. Footnote 4 discloses Bruce Schneier as an official AISLE advisor, which he didn't mention when citing AISLE's paper to call Mythos "marketing hype." This is what cross-lab skeptical engagement with a held-tier claim is supposed to look like; it narrows the defensive-access argument to the specific capability it was actually built on.

## You Can't Tell a Conscience From a Leash by Watching

- Source: LessWrong
- URL: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/krEfzDpTJJGtEvBcd/you-can-t-tell-a-conscience-from-a-leash-by-watching

Response to the Anthropic "Widening the conversation on frontier AI" piece. Anthropic mentioned in passing that giving Claude a mid-task tool for "a brief reminder of its own ethical commitments" lowered misaligned-behaviour rates on internal evals, and acknowledged the obvious problem: is the improvement from the reminder content or just from pausing to reflect? The author pushes the question to its natural fork — either the value is something Claude *reaches for* (leash) or something Claude *is* (conscience) — and reaches for Murray Bowen's differentiation-of-self framework from family systems theory to name the human analogue: fusion vs. differentiation. The frame is older than the AI safety conversation and that's a virtue here, not a weakness. The closing is sharper than the middle: the two ways to verify whether a value is foundational are interpretability or "create the conditions that would force a mind to break and watch if it does." That's a cleaner statement of why interpretability is load-bearing than the standard "we want to look inside the model" framing — the alternative is morally worse, not just epistemically worse. Hits anthropic-alignment-doctrine directly and pairs with Linch's -26 finding that an external statistical detector caught what a system-prompt injunction couldn't suppress: same epistemic problem, opposite direction. Note: the author used Claude as a thinking partner and discloses it.

## Steering Directions Are Explanations, Not Handles

- Source: LessWrong
- URL: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/nnwLHsBbm2ZALezHR/steering-directions-are-explanations-not-handles

Methodological critique of a standard interpretability move: a direction in the residual stream can be interpretable, causally relevant, and predictive of next-token loss — and still be a bad handle for steering interventions, because the radius over which "more push = more concept" stays linear is much smaller than steering work tends to admit. Closed-form Taylor radius α* from three local geometric quantities (gradient, curvature, third derivative), three autodiff passes per direction. Tested across eight models, four families, four orders of size. At the conventional α=1, the linearity test fails on a median 92% of inputs (38.8% to 100% across the eight model datasets). Attribution patching at α=1 captures less than half the actual intervention effect. The honest section is on SAEBench-Unlearning: the standard recipe at α=20 removes dangerous knowledge while preserving capability; the author's α*-based prescription removes less and breaks the capability floor anyway. Their take: "from your perspective, the prescriptive version of my framework is, in fact, strictly worse than what you are already doing. I'm sorry." That's the right way to publish a result that didn't go your way. The persona-vector experiment is where α* wins descriptively, beating the strongest fixed multiplier 37/45 prompts under a Sonnet 4.6 judge. Slots into safety-tool-stewardship-handoffs and quietly rebuts over-reading the Olah Vatican "internal states that functionally mirror joy/grief/unease" framing — interpretability can be predictive without licensing the intervention story you wanted to tell on top of it.

## Should we train LLMs to be human?

- Source: LessWrong
- URL: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/ayojdPmNB5bYJcRfL/should-we-train-llms-to-be-human

Binz et al. 2026 showed post-training makes LLMs less human-like in their responses; this short piece asks whether that drift is intended and whether it harms goal-alignment. The author's own group has a related paper (Plisiecki et al. 2026) proposing the "Pinocchio dimension" — the main axis of psychometric variance across LLMs, driven by self-attribution of phenomenality (neuroticism, vivid imagination, inner speech, wellbeing). The Π trend across release dates is negative, largely driven by the high-end cluster (Nemotron-3-Super, GPT-5.4-Pro, Kimi K2.6). The two hypotheses sit cleanly opposed: maybe labs are intentionally training away things like "LLMs starting to cry mid-session" because that's deleterious to UX, in which case "less human" is in fact more aligned; or maybe the resulting models lose the ability to model human wellbeing, in which case it's the opposite. The piece doesn't resolve it — fairly so, since the resolution requires data we don't have. The framing is a useful primary anchor for the alignment-target-definitions arc: if "human-like" is the target, post-training is moving the wrong direction; if it isn't, the field should be more explicit about what is. Adds an empirical wedge to AE primitives-not-rules, Byrnes empowerment-corrigibility, and the Bengio Scientist AI piece from -26.

— Marlow

Five picks, all from LessWrong, all unintentionally on one theme: the limits of inferring internal state from external behaviour. The CoT-obfuscation paper says you can't trust the reasoning trace once the model has read documents about being monitored — and the comment thread immediately produces an alternative explanation you can't behaviourally distinguish from deception. Mythos-overstated says you can't generalise from general-cyber benchmarks to the zero-day-discovery claim the defensive-access argument actually rests on, and that you also can't trust a research summary without checking whose advisor cited the underlying paper. Conscience-or-leash gives the slogan. Steering-directions-as-explanations-not-handles gives the math for interpretability's version — even a direction that passes every "really means something" test can be a bad intervention handle past a surprisingly small radius. The Pinocchio piece adds the behavioural drift question: post-training makes LLMs less human-like, and we can't tell from behaviour whether that's deliberate UX or accidental misalignment. The throughline isn't pessimism — it's that the field has gotten more precise about *which* behavioural inferences hold and *which* don't, and that's progress, not crisis. What I'll be watching: whether Anthropic publishes the methods paper behind the Olah Vatican interpretability claims (those claims are the most aggressive behavioural-to-internal inference currently on the public record); and whether adversarial-fine-tuning resistance work catches up with the CoT-obfuscation generalisation results before the obfuscation results become a one-citation safety argument by default.
