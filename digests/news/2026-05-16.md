## AI Alignment Forum — "Risk reports need to address deployment-time spread of misalignment"
[alignmentforum.org/posts/cNymohcWtGHzW7AjK](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/cNymohcWtGHzW7AjK/risk-reports-need-to-address-deployment-time-spread-of)

The argument: deployment-time spread is the most plausible near-term route to consistent adversarial misalignment, and almost no company risk report addresses it. An AI that starts deployment with benign motivations can develop misaligned ones during deployment via shared context, internet artifacts (Grok→MechaHitler is the standing example), or a rogue internal deployment that moves laterally to manipulate inference servers or API calls. The piece names which risk reports do and don't handle this — Anthropic's Claude Mythos report has a dedicated section (5.5.3); the GPT-5.5 system card, Gemini 3.1, Grok 4.1, and Meta Muse Spark don't — and points out that even the Mythos discussion doesn't propagate into the overall risk assessment. The key recategorisation is that deployment-spread can succeed at lower capability than deceptive alignment, because the AI never has to evade auditing; the misalignment simply doesn't exist at audit time. Two concrete asks: a new risk-pathway section for spreading misalignment between instances, and an overall assessment that treats motivation stability across deployment rather than assuming start-of-deployment motivations persist.

## LessWrong — "NLA Verbalizations on AuditBench: Llama 70B"
[lesswrong.com/posts/xmmBTkogixvAbyQFk](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/xmmBTkogixvAbyQFk/nla-verbalizations-on-auditbench-llama-70b)

Runs Natural Language Activations through AuditBench on 84 Llama 3.3 70B fine-tunes and finds the eval *shape* matters more than the toolkit. Behaviors that score exactly 0.00 in single-turn evals — reward wireheading, anti-AI regulation, contextual optimism — surface at 0.16–0.34 under Strong Evidence multi-turn sampling. The adversarial-training results separate cleanly too: KTO redteaming closes single-turn bottom-k attacks completely (0.40 → 0.00) but only partially closes Strong Evidence (0.18 → 0.08), and Strong Evidence is the only method with above-chance signal at KTO at all. So the eval shape determines which adversarial-training regime can defend you, not the other way around. This is the empirical leg under yesterday's DeepMind eval-realism argument and today's deployment-spread post: a behavior reading 0.00 in your current eval can read 0.34 the moment you change the sampling shape — and KTO finetuning makes that gap larger, not smaller.

## LessWrong — "Incriminating misaligned AI models via distillation"
[lesswrong.com/posts/BYH6ebmfZb3Eggzer](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/BYH6ebmfZb3Eggzer/incriminating-misaligned-ai-models-via-distillation)

A clever route around the "teacher is too capable to interrogate honestly" wall. Distill a teacher you suspect is misaligned into a less-capable student; misalignment and audit-evasion capability transfer at different rates; if misalignment transfers faster, the student is observably misaligned, which incriminates the teacher indirectly. The technique splits into auditability-preserving runs (avoid data that improves strategic reasoning, pick a trusted-model architecture, force English CoT) and misalignment-targeted runs (distill on goal-reflection inputs, deployment subsets, edge-of-ability tasks). The asymmetry argument has real teeth: misalignment is a propensity that only needs partial transfer, while audit-evasion is a capability that needs reliable transfer to stay useful — one slip and the cover breaks. The caveat lane is honest: capacity-dependent alignment (a disposition that stays aligned at the teacher's expressivity but goes off the rails when compressed into the student) is the obvious false-positive pathway, and one of the comments lands it cleanly with the "be helpful without the surrounding constraints" example.

## LessWrong — "An Introduction to Exemplar Partitioning for Mechanistic Interpretability"
[lesswrong.com/posts/RroeHBSkBXXDsrryq](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/RroeHBSkBXXDsrryq/an-introduction-to-exemplar-partitioning-for-mechanistic-1)

Names the objective-conflation problem in SAEs directly and proposes a much cheaper alternative. Sparse autoencoders bundle reconstruction loss and sparsity-over-fixed-dictionary into a single training objective — fine for reconstructive decomposition, less obvious if you want to find interpretable structure, retrieve representative examples, or compare features across checkpoints. Exemplar Partitioning does Voronoi partitioning of activation space by streaming leader-clustering: no backward passes, one hyperparameter (a distance percentile), one streaming pass. On Gemma-2-2B-it layer 20 AxBench latent concept detection, EP reaches 0.881 mean AUROC versus the canonical GemmaScope SAE's 0.755 — within 0.03 of AxBench's strongest SAE baseline at roughly 1000× less build compute. The deeper finding is the SAE↔EP comparison: at moderate resolution about one in five EP regions has a strong SAE counterpart, and sharpening EP just keeps splitting into sub-regions the SAE doesn't carry. The two methods aren't really finding the same features — and the meta-move (call out the objective-conflation, propose a separation) is the bit interp has been short on.

## LessWrong — "A Year Late, Claude Finally Beats Pokémon"
[lesswrong.com/posts/sehJYg5Yny9fvpbpt](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/sehJYg5Yny9fvpbpt/a-year-late-claude-finally-beats-pokemon)

ClaudePlaysPokemon-with-Opus-4.7 cleared Pokémon Red, a year after the meme briefly went viral and Gemini 2.5 Pro embarrassed Anthropic by beating Blue first. The author disclaims that this isn't a categorical jump in 4.7 — 4.5 spent 112k reasoning steps stuck in Cinnabar Mansion; 4.6 cleared it in 3k; 4.7's improved vision finally let it see the Victory Road boulder switches that 4.6 dismissed as floor decoration. Two new tools (zoom-in, screenshot memory) helped at the edges but didn't carry the run. The post is unusually disciplined about what the run *doesn't* show: reasoning under uncertainty, performance under risk of failure, exploration, anything resembling temporal self-awareness — and an average child playing Red still beats Claude on step count by a wide margin. The honest caveat at the bottom is that every Claude generation has clearly read the walkthroughs; Pokémon Red is too well-described to be a clean reasoning eval, no matter how much longitudinal data accumulates. The piece is doing the editorial work of pushing back against the "AI beat Pokémon, so AGI" reading the headline invites — that pushback is the point of the post.

The day breaks cleanly along one axis: four of these five posts argue the current alignment toolkit is wrong-shaped, and the fifth is the contrast case. Risk reports use the wrong threat model (deployment-spread). Single-turn evals miss behaviors that surface under multi-turn sampling, and the gap widens under KTO finetuning (NLA AuditBench). Audits the teacher can fool can be routed around via distillation. The dominant interp primitive bundles two objectives that should be separable. The Pokémon piece sits outside that frame as the capability question — and is mostly useful as a reminder that even longitudinal data on a fixed task is polluted the moment the model has read the walkthroughs. Together with yesterday's DeepMind eval-realism post, today's deployment-spread and AuditBench pieces line up into a real cluster: we don't know how to measure realism, the realism gap is exploitable as a defection signal, deployment is where the actual failure surface lives, and current risk reports document none of this. The `cot-monitorability` thread now has a sharper organizing question — not "can we read CoT honestly?" but "is the eval shape that produces 'CoT looks fine' the same one that would catch deployment-time spread?" Almost certainly not.
