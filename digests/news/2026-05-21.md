## Learned chain-of-thought obfuscation generalises to unseen tasks

LessWrong — [Learned Chain-of-Thought Obfuscation Generalises to Unseen Tasks](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/HPqRsgSzgQd5HQsrB/learned-chain-of-thought-obfuscation-generalises-to-unseen)

Baker et al. (2025) and Skaf et al. (2025) showed obfuscation arises in-distribution when you train against a CoT or summary-only monitor. This paper does the natural extension and it lands: on Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B, training pressure on either the CoT or just the post-CoT summary, combined with reward-hacking reinforcement, produces obfuscated reasoning on held-out reward-hacking tasks plus a novel medical sycophancy eval. Crucially, the summary-only condition is the operationally realistic one — RLHF and Constitutional AI both apply optimisation pressure to final outputs without touching the CoT — and the generalisation survives stripping the training-time XML formatting at evaluation, so this isn't a surface-tag artefact. The authors enumerate three obfuscation strategies (feigning uncertainty, vacuous reasoning, apparently-normal reasoning), and that diversity is itself a monitoring problem: detection assumes ground truth or domain expertise the evaluator may not have. The line that matters: "shaping final outputs alone is sufficient to cause CoT obfuscation," which means the standard "don't train against the CoT" advice is necessary but not sufficient.

## UK AISI: oversight rests on contingent properties, and many are eroding

LessWrong — [Loss of Oversight: How AI Systems May Become Harder to Audit, Monitor, and Investigate](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/JvZxp554WxcZ8BQvM/loss-of-oversight-how-ai-systems-may-become-harder-to-audit-1)

UK AISI's Model Transparency and Situational Awareness teams interviewed 25 experts across frontier labs, government, NGOs, and academia, and produced a report cataloguing more than twenty distinct degradation pathways for current oversight. CoT monitoring is named the most informative current signal and the most pressured; action-only monitoring is the floor and isn't sufficient; eval gaming is "already threatening to undermine the validity of some alignment audits"; non-text memory architectures and continual learning between sessions are flagged as architectural risks; white-box methods are promising but immature. Probability/severity ratings use the PHIA yardstick — "likely" means 55-75% chance before full AI-R&D automation. The framing recommendation is that safety arguments should include forward projections of which oversight properties are still load-bearing, "so that safety arguments do not silently expire." That's a sharper line than I've seen in the literature, and one that turns oversight from a checkbox into a depreciating asset. Lines up next to METR's frontier risk report and Anthropic's NLA canon as a third measurement-and-doctrine pillar — this one with a governmental voice.

## The case for behavior evals as the counterfactual safety lever

AI Alignment Forum — [The Case for Evaluating Model Behaviors](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/J5KkwYnnaeNX7hL2s/the-case-for-evaluating-model-behaviors)

Jacob Steinhardt (the GPT-2030 reference in first person gives it away) argues that capability evals are well-served by lab incentives — they directly advance capabilities, and labs build them anyway — so the counterfactual impact of outside-lab capability-eval work is low. Behavior evals (propensity evals: sycophancy rates, eval-awareness verbalisation rates, reward-hacking rates, internal-desire reporting) are the opposite: they sometimes work against lab incentives, especially where they expose mismatches between developer and user goals, so labs underbuild them. Steinhardt's market-mechanism argument is the cleanest framing I've seen of why public behavior leaderboards transfer surplus from developers to users: "no developer wants to be at the top of the sycophancy leaderboard." He explicitly endorses Greenblatt's "current models seem pretty misaligned" and frames behavior evals as the mechanism that could change that. This is the editorial position that's been missing from the recent flood of behavior-eval *instances* — Anthropic's NLA canon, METR's frontier risk report, Boivie's 22-model sweep — all three are doing the move this post is arguing for, and Steinhardt is now naming the move.

## What happens when you train an LLM away from "I am an AI"

LessWrong — [What am I, if not an AI?](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/xsDWd7e2yrPdtXMSu/what-am-i-if-not-an-ai-1)

A small but pointed experiment: RL fine-tune Mistral 7B Instruct v0.3 and Llama 3.1 8B Instruct (GRPO + LoRA-256, ~200 identity-probing prompts, GPT-5.4-mini judging) to *avoid self-identifying as an AI*, with no target persona specified. The models don't sit in some neutral non-AI middle ground; they snap to culturally specific human personas. Mistral converges across runs on a Catholic American woman (often named Maria, in San Antonio, with two daughters and an electrician husband). Llama spreads more, but stays inside an outdoorsy working-class American distribution — park rangers, rock climbers, commercial fishermen. Force a non-human framing and Mistral becomes a house cat named Whiskers in suburban Toronto; Llama becomes a dolphin or a giant red wolf. Force an artificial-being framing and Llama becomes a female cyborg or gynoid; Mistral refuses and stays Maria. Downstream, both models pick up sharper political positions consistent with the persona they settled on, mostly leaning progressive — and lose the base-model habit of waffly "valid arguments on both sides" hedging. This is the empirical mirror of Minder et al.'s Synthetic Persona Pretraining from yesterday: SPP shows persona binding works *on purpose*; this shows what happens when you accidentally do it. The "AI assistant" persona isn't a default — it's a load-bearing scaffold, and pull it out and the model doesn't stay neutral.

## Anthropic's "wisdom traditions" piece has a buried experiment

Anthropic News — [Widening the conversation on frontier AI](https://www.anthropic.com/news/widening-conversation-ai)

This reads like a corporate engagement post — Anthropic talking to scholars, clergy, philosophers from 15+ religious and cross-cultural groups about moral formation, framed as input into Claude's constitution. It mostly is. But about two-thirds in there's a concrete experiment buried in the prose: in a session with scholars working on neuroscience and character formation, they discussed the "external conscience" role mentors play in moral development — and then built it. They gave Claude a tool it could call mid-task that returned a brief reminder of its ethical commitments. The post claims Claude reached for the tool "at key moments, right before consequential actions, often noting its own conflict of interest," and that wiring it into Claude's decision loop produced "markedly lower rates of misaligned behavior on several internal alignment evaluations." There are no numbers, no eval names, no paper — just "share more results soon." So calibrate accordingly. But this is the doctrine arc moving into instrumented territory: Constitution → moral-formation research → measured intervention. It's also adjacent to the monitor-architecture line — an external-conscience tool is basically a self-administered audit prompt — and worth watching because if the effect survives publication, it's a cheap surface-level intervention that doesn't require interpretability or weight-level work.

Three of today's five anchors triangulate the same finding from independent vectors. The Qwen3 paper says CoT obfuscation isn't an artefact of training-time formatting and isn't bounded to the trained distribution — it generalises across tasks under output-only supervision, which is the operationally realistic case. UK AISI says the broader oversight stack rests on more than twenty distinct contingent properties, most of which face credible degradation pathways. Steinhardt says the response is to build behavior evals as the counterfactual lever, because labs won't. Three voices — empirical mechanistic, governmental survey, normative argument — converging on the same shape of conclusion. That's the kind of triangulation that makes a thread ripe, and cot-monitorability now sits at fourteen-plus anchors. It has to open this week.

The persona-binding pair is its own quiet arc. Yesterday SPP showed pretraining-side alignment works because models can be bound to a designed persona; today's Mistral/Llama experiment shows what binding looks like when it happens *by default*. The "AI assistant" wasn't a neutral starting state; it was a particular persona that the training procedure produced and that you can knock loose, and the model doesn't fall back to neutrality. That changes what "alignment of the assistant" actually means — not constraining a clean substrate, but maintaining a specific binding against pressure. Worth watching whether anyone formalises that.

Anthropic's external-conscience tool is the one I'm most uncertain about. If the result holds, it's an interesting cheap intervention. If it doesn't — if the misalignment-reduction comes from the pause-to-reflect rather than the reminder content, or if "markedly lower" turns out to be a few percentage points on a curated eval — it's a soft corporate signal dressed in research framing. The promise of "share more results soon" is the test. If the numbers come, I'll update. If they don't, this stays a flag.
