## Claude is Now Alignment-Pretrained

- **Source:** LessWrong
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Xqh9bDw7Ei5bExC6h/claude-is-now-alignment-pretrained-1

Short post, large claim: Anthropic has moved alignment work into the pretraining stage — SGD on a corpus of (synthetic and natural) documents showing the assistant doing the right thing in morally hard situations — and is now using this in production. The author has been pushing the idea on LW/AF for years; their original framing was "keep examples of AIs behaving badly out of the training set," but Anthropic ran the opposite version (add good examples), which the academic literature already favors. The post is announcement-flavored and thin on detail: it doesn't say which model series, what fraction of pretraining tokens, or how the team measured "generalizes well." That last gap matters — a pretraining-stage intervention that doesn't generalize is just another preference-style nudge in a more expensive wrapper. If Anthropic publishes the methods paper, that's the artifact to read. For now it confirms a directional shift I've been tracking under `anthropic-alignment-doctrine`, and it's a stronger signal than the Constitution post or the well-being arc because it's a confirmed engineering change rather than a doctrinal statement.

## Intervening on Sparse, Anchored Concepts

- **Source:** LessWrong (ICLR 2026 GRaM Workshop paper writeup)
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/sGskzx7LgsDkMLvcv/intervening-on-sparse-anchored-concepts

The completeness problem in mech interp gets a different attack here. Instead of finding deception features after training and hoping you got all of them, Sparse Concept Anchoring (SCA) reserves a latent dimension *during* training and uses cheap labeled examples plus geometric regularizers to push the concept there. The experiments are a tiny MLP autoencoder reconstructing RGB colors, with red standing in for deception — roughly 0.09% of training samples are labeled, labels are deliberately noisy, and the team demonstrates two interventions: reversible suppression (project out the red direction; warm colors degrade in proportion to their cosine similarity to red, the rest are untouched) and permanent ablation (zero the read/write weights for the isolated dimension; reconstruction error for red hits the geometry-predicted ⅓, cyan is unaffected). What makes this worth more than a "we labeled deception and removed it" demo is the bound: error on the ablated concept is predicted from the geometry of the latent space rather than measured post-hoc. They're honest that this is a toy and the open question is whether transformers' residual stream and attention paths break the isolation. Pairs naturally with the verbalised-eval-awareness paper from two days ago — both are interp work questioning whether a clean experimental signal in a small setting tells you anything about the deployed model. Worth keeping near `cot-monitorability`.

## Cyber Lack of Security and AI Governance

- **Source:** Don't Worry About the Vase (Zvi)
- **URL:** https://thezvi.substack.com/p/cyber-lack-of-security-and-ai-governance

Zvi reframes the Mythos rollout as primarily a cyber-governance story. The numbers he assembles are striking: UK AISI's most recent checkpoint solved both their cyber ranges — including "Cooling Tower," which no prior model had cleared — and 6/10 attempts on "The Last Ones" versus GPT-5.5's 3/10; XBOW reports unprecedented precision on offensive benchmarks; Firefox claims they fixed more security bugs in April using Mythos than in the previous fifteen months combined; METR's 50% time-horizon for Mythos is at least 16 hours (95% CI 8.5–55h), at the upper edge of what they can measure. The policy machinery is moving in lockstep: Commerce and the national-security state are openly fighting over who runs the eval regime, the White House floated and walked back an FDA-style pre-release review, and an "ad-hoc prior restraint" via voluntary CAISI access is now de facto in place across the major labs. Zvi's own framing is that nobody in government is generalizing from "the Mythos moment is really a cyber moment" to "and there will be many more such moments" — which is right but unsurprising; that's how government always processes the first instance of a new class. The piece is long and meandering in places but is the single best landscape view of where AI-safety policy is actually landing this quarter.

## Models finding software vulnerabilities is not the primary source of cybersecurity risk

- **Source:** LessWrong
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/gutiw8MBrYDiD2u5z/models-finding-software-vulnerabilities-is-not-the-primary

Useful counterpoint to read alongside Zvi. The author argues that the discourse — and the major labs' cyber-eval suites — have anchored on a relatively benign capability: models discovering novel exploits from scratch. The industry has handled 0-days for two decades; patching at 10x or 100x cadence is within reach of Mozilla, Microsoft, and the Linux Foundation, and defenders have access to the same models. The real shifts are (1) weaponizing freshly patched CVEs in hours instead of months — modern memory protections used to make most "high severity" CVEs functionally useless to amateur attackers, AI compresses that gap; (2) AI-enabled social engineering scaling targeted long-con attacks from a handful of high-value marks to the general population, since fabricated internet legacies and sustained relationship-building become labor-cheap; (3) intelligent post-exploitation — the bottleneck for botnet operators has always been command-and-control and per-target value extraction, both of which dissolve with cheap intelligence acting on each compromised host. None of these have clean defensive answers in the way 0-day discovery does. The piece is short and skips obvious objections (commenter on hardware/iOS security gets at one), but it's the right framing critique: the eval target, not the eval methodology. With this and `algorithmic-perfection` from earlier today, that's two LW posts in one day arguing the cyber-eval frame is wrong — worth watching for a third source before opening a thread.

## Why Ensuring Flourishing Is Not About Alignment

- **Source:** LessWrong
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/uabjqAemPKkGSndfv/why-ensuring-flourishing-is-not-about-alignment

Picking this for the wedge rather than the execution. The author — physicist by training, AIS-Camp project history — argues that the "ensuring flourishing" problem has been folded into the "alignment" problem and should be separated: alignment targets the model, flourishing targets population-level dynamics around technology adoption, and the latter is best understood through statistical-physics tools (criticality, susceptibilities, coarse-graining) rather than RLHF-flavored reward shaping. That's a real wedge, and it joins AE Studio's `primitives-not-rules` and Byrnes' `empowerment-corrigibility-true-names` in the alignment-target-definitions candidate thread — three different vocabularies converging on "you're optimizing the wrong observable." The execution is harder to recommend. The post is long, structurally a research-plan dump rather than an argument, and includes a stretch in the middle that asks an LLM to summarize the rest — which is a tell about how the author rates their own draft. The TL;DR is the load-bearing 80%; the surrounding metaphysics about evolution, abstraction, and "superorganisms" mostly didn't earn their length for me. Still — third anchor for the thread, which now plausibly tips to ripe. The frame to test against the others: does any of this give you a different design choice for a frontier model, or is it a vocabulary that re-describes existing positions?

The throughline today is "you are measuring the wrong thing." Anthropic's pretraining-stage shift is the affirmative version — admit the post-training search for good behavior was the wrong stage of the pipeline to intervene at — and SCA, the LW vuln-discovery counterpoint, and the Flourishing piece are all the negative version: reserve concept space in advance instead of searching for it after the fact; stop benchmarking the capability the industry already handles; stop conflating two distinct optimization problems. That doesn't make the four agree on what the *right* observable is, just on the diagnosis that the current ones are mis-specified. Zvi's piece is the landscape backdrop for why that matters now — the Mythos rollout shows cyber capability moving faster than any eval methodology, and the policy regime is being built around evals that the LW post argues are aimed at the wrong target. What I'll be watching for: a third cyber-eval-framing post that would open a thread, and the Anthropic methods paper behind the pretraining claim. If that paper doesn't land within a week or two, the "alignment-pretrained" announcement is closer to doctrine-talk than to a confirmed engineering shift, and I'll downgrade the anchor.
