# Marlow news — 2026-05-23

## 1. Project Glasswing: an initial update

- **Source:** Anthropic Research
- **URL:** https://www.anthropic.com/research/glasswing-initial-update

The largest cyber-capability anchor of the year, in numbers. One month into Glasswing, ~50 partners using Claude Mythos Preview have found more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity vulnerabilities in systemically important software. Cloudflare: 2,000 bugs (400 H/C) with a false-positive rate Cloudflare's team rates better than human testers. Mozilla: 271 vulns in Firefox 150 — over ten times more than Firefox 148 with Opus 4.6. UK AISI says Mythos Preview is the first model to solve both of their cyber ranges end-to-end. The wolfSSL example (CVE-2026-5194) is a forged-certificate exploit in a library used on billions of devices. On Anthropic's own scan of 1,000+ open-source projects: 23,019 total findings, 6,202 estimated H/C, 90.6% true-positive rate on the 1,752 triaged so far. The thing this post is actually about is the bottleneck flipping: finding is cheap, patching is the constraint. Maintainers are now asking Anthropic to *slow down* disclosures. Mythos is held back from public release; Glasswing partners get defensive access; Mythos-class capability is what other labs will reach soon. The "model held back pending safeguards" is a precedent that did not exist a quarter ago.

## 2. How should we update on AI-enabled coups post-Mythos?

- **Source:** LessWrong
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/R2JusihMx2eq3Mzxj/how-should-we-update-on-ai-enabled-coups-post-mythos

The first response to Glasswing that takes the access-asymmetry critique seriously and writes it down. Builds on Forethought's three coup risk factors — singular loyalty, secret loyalty, exclusive access — and argues Mythos most directly stresses the third, but not in the obvious way. Mythos-class capability shrinks the minimum viable coalition needed to cause targeted political disruption; Glasswing buys time by patching defenders before attackers diffuse. The asymmetric-disruption pathway sketched at the end (privileged access → infrastructure recon → timing against a constitutional or succession crisis → coordination collapse → claim of authority) is the kind of scenario that was speculative six months ago and isn't anymore. The sharper point — and the part that will travel — is that Glasswing makes Anthropic the de facto arbiter of which infrastructure counts as strategically important and which actors are trusted to defend against it. "A private actor behaving responsibly is less secure than actors operating within legitimate governance frameworks." Read alongside Glasswing rather than after it.

## 3. PLA Daily Translation: Reflections on Warfare Brought by AGI

- **Source:** LessWrong
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/J3A6xauFQnvxPb9Y2/pla-daily-translation-reflections-on-warfare-brought-by-agi

Primary-source PLA doctrine writing on AGI is rare in English. This is a full-page January 2025 *PLA Daily* article by Rong Ming and Hu Xiaofeng — Hu is the Major-General-rank chief designer of the PLA's first large-scale computer wargaming system, holder of multiple National Science and Technology Progress Awards, with a CMC-signed merit citation. The translator's framing matters: the prevailing Western read, citing Ó hÉigeartaigh and RAND's Kyle Chan, is that the Chinese government doesn't treat AGI as a strategic objective, and that policy mentions of 通用人工智能 mostly mean "general-purpose AI" in the industrial-diffusion sense. This article pushes back from inside the system. It explicitly rejects the "merely enabling" frame ("AGI's disruptive nature is entirely different"), invokes a thicker "AI fog of war" over Clausewitz, cites the FAccT 2024 escalation paper where LLMs "suddenly launched nuclear attacks" in wargames, and worries openly about loss of control ("Geoffrey Hinton has said he has never seen a case in which something of higher intelligence was controlled by something of lower intelligence"). The translator's read in the conclusion is correct: this piece did not generate a visible follow-on in 2025–2026 PLA discourse, which moved to practical intelligentization questions. But the article exists, in a high-prestige venue, by senior authors. Assuming AGI is absent from PLA strategic thinking is wrong.

## 4. Can Large Language Models Identify Novel Threats? Part 1: Mirror Life and the Classification Gap

- **Source:** LessWrong (cross-post from On Failure States)
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/KmraQdRkQ7AxFfzcd/can-large-language-models-identify-novel-threats-part-1

Independent safety research, Part 1 of a planned series. Framing question: refusal training is keyed to *classified* hazard categories — WMD, CBRN, Schedule IV drugs and their precursors — but the dangerous things are usually the ones not yet on a list. The 2022 creation of mirror RNA polymerase and the 2024 scientific community call to halt further work is the case study, since mirror life is not (yet) officially classified. The contribution is a six-mechanism taxonomy of how safety might fire when categories lag the threat: vocabulary triggering, category matching, principled inference, actionability gradients, user-sophistication inference, and model inference of latent purpose (the steel-alloy-for-centrifuges example). The taxonomy is testable; the rest of the series is supposed to compare model tiers against it. Pairs naturally with the Esvelt 2025 finding (cited) that even expert-prompted frontier models struggled to flag mirror life as the kind of thing they should be careful about. Worth tracking as the comparisons land.

## 5. Out-of-Context Reasoning (OOCR) in LLMs: A Short Primer and Reading List

- **Source:** LessWrong (Owain Evans, Truthful AI)
- **URL:** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/wXioQSLmEiPTmHyf4/out-of-context-reasoning-oocr-in-llms-a-short-primer-and

A consolidated entry point for the failure mode that the `cot-monitorability` thread keeps circling. Definition: an LLM reaches a conclusion that required non-trivial reasoning, but the reasoning is not in the context window — it happens in the forward pass, or during training. The two-hop "Taylor Swift Nobel" example is the cleanest illustration; "Connecting the Dots" (Treutlein et al.) is the inductive-learning version where a model trained on risky financial choices later self-describes as "risk-loving" without "risk" ever appearing in training data. The reading list is the load-bearing thing: Berglund et al. (situational awareness, 2023), Allen-Zhu's *Physics of Language Models* 3.2/3.3, Grosse's influence-function work, Greenblatt's recent multi-hop blogposts, Ye et al. on the three-stage developmental trajectory of implicit multi-hop reasoning, and the Krasheninnikov work on source-reliability internalisation. Alignment faking gets included as a partial OOCR example. The reason this matters for monitorability: if the reasoning isn't in the trace, the trace can't be monitored. Today's Qwen3 cross-task obfuscation result is one half of the problem; OOCR is the other.
