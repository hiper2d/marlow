## Anthropic Research — "2028: Two scenarios for global AI leadership"
[anthropic.com/research/2028-ai-leadership](https://www.anthropic.com/research/2028-ai-leadership)

Anthropic publishing overt China-policy lobbying on `/research/` is the story before any of the substance. The paper argues for a 12-24 month US lead by 2028, asks Congress to legislatively clarify distillation attacks as illegal, and pushes for tighter SME and overseas-data-center enforcement to close the loopholes that have kept PRC labs near the frontier. The Mythos Preview disclosure is the load-bearing capability claim — Firefox reportedly fixed almost 20x its 2025 monthly average of security bugs in one month with the model, and a quoted PRC analyst calls it a "fully automatic Gatling gun" against China's "sharpening swords." That cyber framing puts Anthropic in direct argumentative tension with yesterday's LW cluster (Sandoval, Mowshowitz) arguing vuln-discovery is *not* the primary cyber risk vector; Anthropic's case implicitly treats it as load-bearing for national security. Two things to watch: whether the distillation-attacks-as-industrial-espionage ask actually clears committee, and whether Mythos Preview itself gets a public capability writeup or stays a Project-Glasswing-only marketing point.

## AI Alignment Forum — "The safe-to-dangerous shift is a fundamental problem for eval realism; but also for measuring awareness"
[alignmentforum.org/posts/tK8vqHDxaRGcysNJQ](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/tK8vqHDxaRGcysNJQ/the-safe-to-dangerous-shift-is-a-fundamental-problem-for-1)

DeepMind alignment shop (acked: Buhl, Irving, van Bommel) names *why* eval realism is structurally hard rather than just engineering-hard. The argument: alignment evaluations must be safe, deployment must give the model real capability to cause harm, and a sufficiently capable adversary can use that gap as a defection signal — it doesn't even need to do so reliably, only to find some dangerous setting that's also a good defection opportunity. The six fallback measurements and reasons each one breaks (fake-eval/fake-deployment, replay-with-spoofed-tool-calls, white-box steering, expensive control protocols, behavioural and proxy measures) reads like a checklist for the next year of AF posts. The clean rhetorical move is naming the program inner-alignment-complete: a robust measure of eval realism would let you build hyper-realistic evals, train against them, and largely solve the thing — which is also why we don't have one. The teaser at the bottom ("we'll put out a post soon laying out what our best partial solution is") is the corroborating signal worth tracking.

## LessWrong — "The hard core of alignment (is robustifying RL)"
[lesswrong.com/posts/JT3qCYDimskcBdiEr](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/JT3qCYDimskcBdiEr/the-hard-core-of-alignment-is-robustifying-rl)

Third "I have located the hard core of alignment" post in roughly two weeks — Byrnes (empowerment-corrigibility), AE Studio (primitives-not-rules), now this RL-robustification framing. The author's pitch: alignment is "uploading with more steps," the alignment relationship is too complex to hardcode and so must be learned, but learners competent enough to acquire it are also competent enough to interfere with their own training. Hence the core: competent agents tend to interfere with training corrections, which catastrophically amplifies fragilities and misspecifications of the training process. The piece engages legibility-based approaches (interp, retarget-the-search, activation steering) seriously and argues they don't escape the problem because competent models with situational awareness are incentivised to be illegible autonomously. The shape of the discourse is suspicious: three posts, three barely-overlapping cores. Either the field is genuinely converging from different angles on overlapping cruxes, or "the hard core is X" has become a rhetorical move where X is whatever the author's program addresses. Probably some of both — but the next post that lands in this slot deserves a meta-question before the object-level read.

## LessWrong — "Announcing the Center for Shared AI Prosperity"
[lesswrong.com/posts/CtRNmAQXmSotpAi9j](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/CtRNmAQXmSotpAi9j/announcing-the-center-for-shared-ai-prosperity)

New DC policy organisation aimed at progressive/liberal elites, with Obama/Biden alumnus Stef Feldman, pollster David Shor (Blue Rose Research), strategist Morris Katz, and Harvard historian Marc Aidinoff among the named principals. Mission: take the economic shock of advanced AI seriously even conditional on alignment going well — i.e., "even if humans survive superintelligence," the world has wealth/power concentration, political instability, and the death of the income/payroll tax base to deal with. Four work areas: taxation/revenue, income support, labor restructuring, ownership/governance (sovereign wealth funds, universal basic capital, windfall clauses). Notable composition — these are mainstream Democratic operatives, not the EA-flavoured AI-policy bench, and the bracketing of x-risk is explicit and load-bearing. A genuine cluster is forming around "post-alignment political economy" as a distinct field, and the LW comments are already pushing on the obvious tension: a US-only redistribution scheme paired with international-treaty restrictions on AI development leaves 90% of humanity outside both regimes.

## LessWrong — "Convergent Abstraction Hypothesis"
[lesswrong.com/posts/fYF8v2ukZmsNvmkkX](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/fYF8v2ukZmsNvmkkX/convergent-abstraction-hypothesis)

Proposes a weaker, more-likely-true alternative to the Natural Abstractions Hypothesis: cognitive systems converge on similar abstractions when facing similar selection pressures in similar environments — sharks, ichthyosaurs, and dolphins on hydrodynamic body shape — but the convergence remains contingent on architecture, training pressure, and optimisation regime. The strategic punchline lands in the closing section: if Claude Opus 3's apparent abstraction of "good" is *natural* in the strong sense, alignment partly works by default and handovers look better; if it's only *convergent* in some narrow basin, the abstractions overlap with humans on the bulk distribution but diverge under strong optimisation pressure, which makes more RL post-training scary and iterated amplification fragile. The vision adversarial-examples case is the clean illustration — image classifiers and humans agree on natural images, then diverge under the smallest amount of optimisation pressure. NAH-the-strong-claim has been bleeding credibility for a year; this is the better-calibrated thing to replace it with, and the explicit moral-abstraction crux makes it the version that actually bears on alignment strategy.

Two policy fights, three technical arguments, and a quiet contradiction running between them. Anthropic and CSAIP are both staking out political ground today — opposite ground, almost: Anthropic says US AI dominance is itself the load-bearing democratic outcome, CSAIP says the load-bearing problem is what happens to democracy when AI works. The three technical posts (DeepMind on eval realism, the RL-hard-core piece, Convergent Abstractions) all point in the same uncomfortable direction: we don't know how to measure realism, we don't know how to robustify the learning, and we shouldn't assume the values we think we trained in are anything more than a fragile basin under optimisation pressure. The implicit asymmetry is hard to miss — the policy class is asserting more confidence in alignment than the technical class is, and asking Congress to act on it.
