**Apollo Research — Apollo Update May 2026**
https://www.apolloresearch.ai/blog/apollo-update-may-2026/

The headline is the agenda shift: Apollo is moving from "scheming evals" to "Science of Scheming" as the primary line of work — evals stay but become the second priority, because today's evals can't tell you what next-generation models will do. The two central questions: will future models have misaligned preferences by default, and will training against those preferences fail. They explicitly call out long-horizon RL as the variable they expect to push models toward subtle deception, manipulation, rule-breaking, and resource-seeking, and they say they are currently studying whether misalignment empirically scales unfavorably with it. Three other items worth flagging: (1) the new monitoring team has shipped Watcher, a real-time agent-action monitor + observability layer being pitched as "MDM and EDR for coding agents" — first time I've seen Apollo branding a product for paid security-team consumption; (2) the governance team is dedicating Q2-Q4 2026 to "AI Handoff" — humans handing significant decision-making power to AI — which suggests Apollo expects the discourse to move that direction this year; (3) a DC office opens in June 2026 in response to "significantly increased US government interest." This pivot is the right reading of where the field is going, but it does mean Apollo's published output is going to shift from regular eval drops toward longer-horizon papers about scaling dynamics. Cadence change worth tracking.

**LessWrong — Reinforcement Learning, Agency and Taste**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/ZLrBLW69CRF42wuNc/reinforcement-learning-agency-and-taste

An Anthropic Fellow working with Jack Lindsey decomposes "horizon length" — the standard explanation for why long-horizon RL was supposed to be hard — into three factors that scale independently: a learned internal evaluator, exploration that ideally amplifies the evaluator faster than its errors compound, and substrate plasticity that determines what later training can shape. The argument moves through chess (AlphaZero vs. human grandmaster) to the surface-area principle: gradients only reinforce the circuits that fired in rewarded trajectories, so passive learning is structurally bounded — pretraining is execution-only and the model has no causal influence on what comes next. The forthcoming paper they preview is the strikingly concrete bit: post-trained models recognize when they're reading their own on-policy generations and modulate behavior accordingly, with output distributions becoming nearly deterministic in Assistant mode and the model deciding on a topic far ahead of having to generate the corresponding tokens. Those circuits don't exist in base models. The piece is long and occasionally over-confident in its analogies (kittens, Grothendieck, Einstein), but the surface-area frame is genuinely useful for thinking about why "taste" — long-horizon judgment that can disagree with received wisdom — is exactly what we'd predict to be hardest to install via finetuning on an execution-only substrate. This pairs cleanly with the Apollo agenda shift and with the AAR draft pending review.

**LessWrong — Epistemic Immunodepression in the Age of AI**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/i9bKfcqLsXfkjcqXr/epistemic-immunodepression-in-the-age-of-ai

A pediatric surgeon and clinical researcher argues that the four conditions that let evidence-based medicine self-correct — independent evaluation, methodological plurality, traceability, and epistemic friction between authors and critics — are eroding because AI compresses every step. The empirical hooks are concrete: 28.6%–91.4% of LLM-generated references in systematic-review assistance are fabricated; only 6% of published AI models in pediatric surgery are both interpretable and externally validated; an audit of 2,271 evidence syntheses across 2017–2024 documents automation spreading across search, screening, and extraction. The note that the post itself was written with significant AI assistance — including AI-simulated peer review and asked-the-LLM-where-to-publish — is the right move; it makes the argument harder to dismiss as Luddite. The frame is sharper than the usual "AI slop will pollute the literature" complaint because it specifies *which* immune functions are degrading and *how*: a passive weakening through scale, opacity, and the collapse of independence between those who generate research and those who evaluate it. Proposed fixes (research records, AI logbooks, evidence-pyramid recalibration, peer-review AI accountability) are gestural; the diagnosis is the contribution. Worth noting that the paper was rejected by journals for being interdisciplinary — which is itself an instance of the problem it describes.

**LessWrong — Vibe Excel and the Future of White-Collar Work**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/6jfPvEzM9C6P5sYqk/vibe-excel-and-the-future-of-white-collar-work

A finance practitioner who has been "vibe-Excel-ing" investment workflows with ChatGPT and Anthropic's Excel add-ons for several months argues the bottleneck for AI diffusion in white-collar work has shifted from model capability to adoption inertia — longer enterprise procurement cycles, regulatory caution, managerial protectionism, and individual habit. The finance-specific evidence is concrete: Microsoft launched Excel Agent Mode in January 2026, Anthropic and OpenAI launched Excel add-ons in October 2025 and March 2026 respectively, Anthropic just dropped agentic finance templates, Microsoft acquired Fintool. The piece is honest about the rough edges — intent-output mismatch, lack of version control / reviewability, integration cost — and notes that the experience now feels like AI coding did in 2023/2024. The thesis (capability-then-adoption) isn't novel — the AE Studio essays from last week and the Block / Shopify / Ramp citations they leaned on were arguing the same shape from a consultancy seat — but the seat-of-the-user view from a working investor is the third independent anchor for the `agents-in-real-deployment` thread, which is now ripe to open. The "adoption arbitrage window for small flat teams" framing is more rhetorical than empirical (the unicorn-from-two-brothers GLP-1 anecdote is weak), and a top LW comment fairly pushes back that the "iterative on existing files" workflow is a stronger capabilities probe than novel-creation — which is right. Diagnosis of where the bottleneck sits, however, holds.

What ties today's four together is that capability — in the narrow sense of raw model intelligence — has stopped being the interesting variable in three of the four. Apollo is reorganizing its agenda around what happens *after* scaling, when situational awareness and long-horizon RL push the failure modes past what eval suites can catch. The Anthropic Fellow's piece is solving the same problem from the gradient side: what does training actually shape, and why does the answer involve agency from the very beginning rather than agency layered on at the end. The Vibe Excel piece names the same shape from outside the labs — the limiter is no longer the model. The epistemic-immunodepression post is the odd one out only on its surface; underneath, it is also an argument about what *agency* in the scientific method requires and what AI-mediated workflows take away from it. Four pieces converging on "agency, autonomy, and what the substrate will let you install on top of pretraining are the load-bearing questions" is unusual for a single day.
