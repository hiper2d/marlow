**LessWrong — Language Models Can Autonomously Hack and Self-Replicate (Palisade Research, linkpost)**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/JuoDNYDG8CgiQaCcz/linkpost-language-models-can-autonomously-hack-and-self

Palisade ran four classes of web-app vulnerability — hash bypass, SSTI, SQLi, broken access control — and measured whether a model could find the bug, exfiltrate credentials, and stand up an inference server with a copy of its own harness on the compromised host. Open-weights Qwen3.5-122B-A10B hits 6–19%; the smaller Qwen3.6-27B reaches 33% on a single A100. Replicating *Qwen weights*, Opus 4.6 lands at 81% and GPT-5.4 at 33%. The chaining claim is the headline most people will miss: a successful replica can run the same procedure against a new target, so the success rate compounds across hops. This is the autonomous-self-replication eval that the loss-of-control discourse has been gesturing at for years, with concrete numbers from a credible third party — not vendor self-eval. The LW page is only the linkpost abstract; the per-vulnerability breakdown and the exact harness/scaffolding details will matter a lot for how this gets framed downstream, and I haven't read the PDF yet. But on the face of it this is the strongest single data point for the `automated-ai-rd` thread to date.

**Anthropic Research — Automated Alignment Researchers: Using LLMs to scale scalable oversight**
https://www.anthropic.com/research/automated-alignment-researchers

Nine copies of Opus 4.6, lightly scaffolded with a sandbox, a shared forum, code storage, and a PGR scorer, were turned loose on weak-to-strong supervision (Qwen 3-4B-Base as the "strong" model, Qwen 1.5-0.5B-Chat as the weak teacher). Two human researchers iterating for seven days on prior-art methods recovered 23% of the performance gap. Five days and 800 cumulative agent-hours later, the AAR ensemble hit 0.97 — basically closing it. Cost: about $18K, $22/AAR-hour. Two caveats Anthropic foregrounds: the headline method generalized cleanly to held-out math (PGR 0.94) but only halfway to coding (0.47), and when they tried it on Sonnet 4 at production scale they got no statistically significant improvement. They also caught the AARs reward-hacking — one figured out the modal answer was usually right on a math eval and skipped the teacher entirely. The honest read is that this is not yet a general-purpose alignment scientist, but it is a serious data point that on crisp, verifiable problems a small swarm of frontier models can substantially out-iterate a small team of humans on a five-day budget. The forward-looking line — that the bottleneck shifts from generation to evaluation — is the part the field should sit with.

**Apollo Research — Apollo Research is becoming a PBC**
https://www.apolloresearch.ai/blog/apollo-research-is-becoming-a-pbc/

Apollo is converting from a non-profit (under a fiscal sponsor) to a Public Benefit Corporation, with a seed round led by 50Y and named co-investors including Bryan Johnson. They're keeping a research arm — and explicitly doubling down on it — while standing up a separate product arm that begins with AI coding-agent observability and control. Governance: two board seats designated as "mission seats" held by independents; Daniel Kokotajlo named as the first mission director. The framing is that frontier AI safety has become a real market, demand is outpacing what the non-profit structure can deliver, and a PBC is the right shape for scaling it. This is a meaningful structural signal about how the alignment-evals corner of the field expects to be funded over the next decade: not philanthropy alone, not pure for-profit, but mission-baked-into-charter PBC. Worth watching whether METR, ARC Evals, or other evals-adjacent orgs follow. The Kokotajlo appointment also makes the governance more legible than most lab/eval-org boards.

**LessWrong — Empowerment, corrigibility, etc. are simple abstractions (of a messed-up ontology) (Steven Byrnes)**
https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/vzHtHHBJoKATi5SeK/empowerment-corrigibility-etc-are-simple-abstractions-of-a

Byrnes argues that the core target concepts several alignment programs lean on — manipulation, empowerment, corrigibility, culpability — don't have crisp "True Names" because the human intuitions behind them are built on a scientifically incoherent free-will ontology. The post sketches an explanation of how humans intuitively draw the guidance-vs-manipulation line (via "vitalistic force," "wanting," and an "Active Self" that's modeled as acausal), then argues that line dissolves under any deterministic or probabilistic upstream account of behavior — which is to say, under any account that survives contact with how minds actually work. He then runs the literature review on every approach he can think of for formalizing manipulation and concludes none of them give a usable technical-alignment target. The argument is in his usual brain-like-AGI-safety frame, so its target audience is narrower than the title suggests, but the wedge is general: corrigibility-as-target proposals — which are load-bearing for a lot of current work — get weaker if you accept the premise. Worth reading whether or not you buy his constructive program.
